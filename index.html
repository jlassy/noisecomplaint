Why these two operators?

Most aircraft over central Toronto pass through once, landing, departing, or heading somewhere else. Tour helicopters are different: the whole business model is repeated loops over the same downtown landmarks, back to base, then up again with the next group. That looping pattern is what generates the repeat noise.

Two operators run this kind of circuit out of Billy Bishop Airport: Toronto Heli Tours and Great Lakes Helicopter. Running multiple loops a day in the same downtown corridor, they account for the large majority of the looping, low-altitude helicopter noise residents hear, as distinct from a one-off medevac, police, or charter flight passing through once.

Not every helicopter overhead is one of these two. But if you're hearing a small helicopter If you hear a small helicopter (commonly a Robinson R44) circling rather than flying through, a tour flight is the most likely explanation. Checking flight tracking takes about a minute and makes your complaint much more specific.

It is not only helicopters. Small fixed-wing aircraft use the same airspace for sightseeing and training, flying the same repeated loops. Because a handful of aircraft can each pass overhead many times per outing, it can feel like a constant stream of different planes. In practice, almost all the noise comes from a small number of operators flying the same circuits.

Health and safety

Health Canada and the WHO link frequent aircraft noise to elevated stress hormones, disrupted sleep, and cardiovascular risk. Helicopters are a particular case. Their low-frequency blade-slap is measurably more disruptive than the same noise from a fixed-wing aircraft, and several regulators apply an extra annoyance penalty to rotorcraft as a result. Repeated overflights are not just unpleasant; the health effects are documented.

On safety: repeated low circuits over residential rooftops put any mechanical failure directly above people's homes rather than over open water. There is no good reason for these routes to be planned this way when the waterfront is right there.

Confirm it yourself before you complain

Naming the right operator, altitude, and time makes a complaint far more credible. For tour helicopters, Flightradar24 is the tool that actually works.

  • Flightradar24 , free site and app. Zoom into the Billy Bishop area; helicopters show as separate icons from airline traffic. Tap one to see the operator, registration, altitude, and speed.
  • WebTrak , Billy Bishop's official tracking tool, free, no login. Built for runway traffic, so it generally doesn't pick up tour helicopters from the separate Polson Pier heliport. Use Flightradar24 for helicopter tours. (Medevac, police, and military flights are excluded from both by federal agreement.)
  • Transport Canada Civil Aircraft Registry , if Flightradar24 shows a registration mark (like C-GPEV or C-GKLD) but the operator name isn't clear, paste it into this free federal database to find the registered owner.

Quick shortcut: Great Lakes Helicopter sticks close to the lake, running east-west no further than Riverdale/Leslieville. Toronto Heli Tours flies much wider loops covering the Annex, Rosedale, and Riverdale/Leslieville. If you are in any of those areas, it is almost certainly Toronto Heli Tours, you can address the complaint to them without checking flight tracking.

Transport Canada (federal regulator)

Transport Canada sets and enforces national noise and airspace rules, but has no public complaints inbox. Contact them by phone or web form. Most useful for repeated or safety-related patterns.
